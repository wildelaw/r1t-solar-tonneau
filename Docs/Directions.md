Here are the complete, finalized engineering instructions for building your **55" x 13 1/2" x 1/2" steel-framed solar panel**. 

This design incorporates the welded steel cross-beams for zero-sag structural support, the "Direct Bond + Expansion Cap" method for permanent metal-to-resin sealing, and the non-sulfur clay method for clean, post-cure junction box wiring on the back of the panel.

---

### Materials List

**Structural & Mold:**
*   **Steel L-Beams:** 54 7/8" x 1/2" x 1/2" (length should be 1/8 shorter than width at shortest point will refer to length as ~55")
*   **Cross-Beams:** 3x Flat steel bars, 13" long, 1/8" thick.
*   **Mold Surface:** 18" x 60" Melamine board.
*   **Epoxy Metal Primer:** Automotive self-etching primer or 2-part epoxy primer.

**Composite & Resin:**
*   **Fiberglass Cloth:** 6oz woven fiberglass cloth (cut to 55" x 13 1/4").
*   **Resin:** UV-Stabilized Clear Marine Epoxy Resin + **Slow Hardener** (2-gallon kit; you will use ~1.2 gallons or 1.131 gallons + buffer).
    *  **Layer 1** - Fiberglass + epoxy resin + black dye: 12 1/2" x 55" x 1/8" = 85.94 cubic inches (resin = 83.79; fiberglass 2.15) which is ~0.363 gallons of resin (about 46.5 fluid ounces)
    *  **Layer 2** - Three cross-beams + epoxy resin: 13 1/4" x 55" x 1/8" = 91.09375 cubic inches (resin = 86.21875 cubic inches; cross-beams = 4.875 cubic inches) which is ~0.374 gallons of resin (about 47.8 fluid ounces)
    *  **Layer 3** - Solar Cells
    *  **Layer 4** - Resin Flood: 13 1/4" x 55" x 1/8" = 91.09375 cubic inches which is ~0.394 gallons of resin (about 50.5 fluid ounces)
*   **Non-Sulfur Modeling Clay:** (e.g., Plastilina or non-sulfur plasteline) for sealing wire pass-throughs.
*   **Marine-Grade Silicone:** For the top expansion cap and J-Box mounting.

**Electrical:**
*   Solar cells (sized to fit inside the 55x13.5 frame).
*   Tabbing wire, bus wire, flux pen, soldering iron.
*   Direct-Solder IP67 Waterproof Junction Box.

**Consumables:**
*   Acetone, 80-grit & 120-grit sandpaper.
*   Heavy-duty packing tape, Kapton tape (for frame insulation).
*   Mixing buckets, stir sticks, squeegees, chip brushes.
*   Butane/Propane torch, drill and drill bits.
*   Warm water, dish soap, Scotch-Brite pad.

### Phase 1: Frame Prep & Bonding Surface

1.  **Weld the Frame:** Weld your steel frame to 55" x 13 1/2" with a 1/2" outer measurements with the 13" cross-beams. Weld the two 13" flat steel cross-beams to the *inside bottom* of the frame, exactly 7" from each short end. Also weld a cross-beam in the center at 27 1/2"
    *  Use clamps and thin strip of hardboard at 13 1/4" as a jig to achieve a squared off frame.

2.  **Flatten the Frame:** Welding warps steel. Place the frame on a perfectly flat table. If it rocks, use a hammer to gently tap it flat before continuing.
3.  **Prep the Metal:** Use 80-grit sandpaper to heavily scuff the *inside* of the steel channel. Wipe it completely clean with acetone.

4.  **Prime the Metal:** Spray the inside of the channel with epoxy metal primer. Let it cure fully (24 hours). Lightly scuff the primer with 120-grit sandpaper and wipe with acetone. *This creates the perfect chemical bond for the Direct Bond method.*

### Phase 2: Bottom-Up Layup & Hole Drilling

1.  **Prep the Mold:** Lay the melamine board flat. Tape the edges of the board with packing tape so it doesn't absorb resin. Place your primed steel frame on top.
2.  **Wet Out the Fiberglass:** Cut the 6oz fiberglass cloth to fit snugly inside the frame. Mix a small batch (~16 oz) of epoxy. Lay the cloth on the melamine and squeegee the epoxy into it until clear. Let this back skin **cure completely hard** (24 hours).
3.  **Remove Amine Blush:** Epoxy produces a waxy byproduct called amine blush as it cures. Wash the cured fiberglass skin with warm water, dish soap, and a Scotch-Brite pad. Dry completely.
4.  **Drill Wire Exits:** Measure exactly 2 inches inward from the steel frame on the cured back skin. Drill two holes just wide enough for your flat bus wire to pass through. 

---

### Phase 3: Cell Placement & Routing

1.  **Insulate the Frame Edge:** Solar cells cannot touch bare steel or they will short out and crack under flex. Run a strip of Kapton or packing tape along the inside bottom lip of the steel frame.
2.  **Lay the Cells (FACE UP):** Place your solar cells **face-up** (blue side facing the ceiling) onto the cured fiberglass skin. 
3.  **Solder Strings:** Solder your cells together in your desired series/parallel configuration.
4.  **Route the Bus Bars:** Solder your main positive and negative flat bus bars to the cell strings. Leave the ends of the bus bars about 4 inches too long. Push these excess ends down *through* the two drilled holes in the back skin so they stick out the bottom of the panel.
5.  **Seal the Holes (The Clay Method):** From the *inside* (top) of the panel, pack non-sulfur modeling clay tightly around the bus wires where they pass through the fiberglass. Build a solid 1-inch mound. *Do not use standard sulfur-based clay, as sulfur inhibits epoxy curing.* 

---

### Phase 4: The Resin Pour (Direct Bond Method)

1.  **Scuff the Skin:** Lightly sand the cured fiberglass skin (around the cells) with 120-grit paper and wipe with acetone. This ensures the new flood coat chemically bonds to the back skin.
2.  **The Seal Coat (Bubbles):** Mix ~16 oz of epoxy. Pour a thin skim coat over the fronts (blue side) of the solar cells. Use a brush to physically dab resin under the edges of the cells to push trapped air out. Wait for this to cure to a gel state (approx 3-4 hours).
3.  **The Flood Coat:** Mix a large batch of epoxy (do not mix more than 32 oz at a time to prevent the bucket from overheating). Pour it into the frame over the front of the cells. 
4.  **The Expansion Gap (CRITICAL):** Pour the resin until it is **1/16" below the top lip of the steel frame**. Do not let the resin touch the top edge. 
5.  **Torch the Bubbles:** Lightly pass a torch over the surface to pop any remaining air bubbles. Keep the torch moving and away from the bus wires.
6.  **Cure:** Let the panel sit perfectly level for 48 hours. The clay mound will prevent any resin from leaking out the bottom holes.

---

### Phase 5: Post-Cure, Expansion Cap, & J-Box Soldering

1.  **Demold:** Lift the panel off the melamine board. Flip it over. You will see the clay mounds on the back, with the flat bus wires sticking out.
2.  **Remove the Clay:** Pick the clay out of the holes. It will peel away cleanly, leaving perfectly sealed, flush holes around the bus wires. 
3.  **The Expansion Cap:** Flip the panel back over (sun-side up). Run a smooth bead of marine silicone into the 1/16" empty gap between the cured resin and the top edge of the steel frame. Tool it smooth with a gloved finger. This flexible cap allows the steel to expand in the heat without cracking the rigid resin.
4.  **Mount the J-Box:** Apply a thick bead of silicone adhesive to the flange of your direct-solder J-Box. Press it onto the back of the fiberglass panel, directly over the two wire exit holes, 2 inches away from the steel frame. 
5.  **Solder:** Trim the flat bus wires to length inside the J-Box and solder them directly to the terminal pads inside the box. Seal the J-Box lid.

### Pro Hints

*   **Slow Hardener is Mandatory:** Pouring ~1 gallon of epoxy into a tight space creates a massive exothermic reaction. If you use fast hardener, the resin will boil, turn milky white, and ruin the panel. 
*   **Batch Pouring:** If you need to pour more than 32 oz at once, pour it in two batches spaced 15 minutes apart. This spreads out the heat generation and prevents flash-boiling in the bucket.
*   **Drill Bit Sizing:** Make the drill holes for the bus bars slightly oversized. Flat bus wires have sharp corners that can chafe against the fiberglass during thermal expansion. A slightly larger hole prevents this.

### Things to Watch Out For

1.  **Amine Blush:** If you skip washing the cured fiberglass back skin with soap and water before pouring the flood coat, the invisible waxy blush will act as a release agent. The flood coat will peel off the back skin later. 
2.  **Sulfur in Clay:** Double-check the packaging on your modeling clay. Sulfurized clay (often used for standard sculpting) will emit gases that stop epoxy from curing for a 2-inch radius around the hole, causing a permanent leak.
3.  **Steel Short Circuits:** Ensure no tabbing wire or cell edges are resting directly against the bare steel frame before you pour. Tape the inside bottom edge of the frame thoroughly.
4.  **Resin Shrinking:** Epoxy shrinks by about 1-2% as it cures. Pouring slightly *under* the steel frame lip (the 1/16" gap) accounts for this shrinkage perfectly and prevents the panel from looking under-filled.
